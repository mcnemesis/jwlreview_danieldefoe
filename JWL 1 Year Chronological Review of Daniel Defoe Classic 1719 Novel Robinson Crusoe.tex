\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a chronological literary review of Daniel Defoe's 1719 classic novel \textit{Robinson Crusoe} by his student and modern novelist, J. Willrich Lutalo, author of 2025 literary fiction novel, \textit{Rock `N' Draw}. It is compiled over an extended period of reflective reading spanning longer than a year; between January 2025 and May 2026. The review is structured as a series of dated notes, each capturing the precise moment of engagement with the text, accompanied by excerpts from the novel and personal commentary. This method allows for a layered exploration of the novel's themes, narrative techniques, and cultural resonances, while also documenting the evolving perceptions of the reader across more than a year of study.  

By situating the review within a temporal framework, the work highlights how sustained, reflective reading can deepen understanding of canonical literature. The inclusion of excerpts alongside commentary provides a dialogic interplay between Defoe's text and contemporary reflection, offering insights into the enduring relevance of \textit{Robinson Crusoe}. Ultimately, this review demonstrates the value of chronological annotation as both a scholarly and creative practice, producing a record that is at once analytical, personal, and historically conscious.  
 \newline\newline
     \textbf{Keywords}: Daniel Defoe, Robinson Crusoe, Reflective Reading, Canonical Literature, Chronological Literary Review
\end{abstract}

Thus, upon taking on a [new] slave or servant, we learn from Crusoe what the important first lessons are; to give the guy a [new] name and also teach him how to correctly refer to his owner or boss --- as ``Master".


\subsection{Later Note}

The [literal] manner of Crusoe's acquisition of this servant aside, it should be worth discussing whether or not the author of the story likewise wished to communicate something else or deeper. First, note the striking similarity between Crusoe's preference of the title ``Master" in relation to his servant or acquired companion. Of course, despite not having paid for the man's life via an exchange of currency as would typically have been the case in ancient slave-acquisition transactions, and yet, because he had saved his life from brutal sudden death, he somewhat had indeed paid for the man's life, and so, it would be right to ascribe to him the status of a slave; a subject or subordinate relative to a ``Master". 

But then, and as is the case with much of Defoe's work, the scriptures likewise come to mind; Jesus, the Saving protagonist in all the gospels, was occasionally referred to as ``Master" by his disciples. However, and as we see in the following note ascribed to Copilot \cite{copilot_assistant}, Jesus had never directly instructed nor commanded his fellows to refer to him by such a title despite being their ultimate savior:

In that bit of the story, and slightly after the reanimated corpse comes under control of the mage, he instructs it to refer to him as ``Master", as a means to properly establish the chain of command between him, the ``Frankenstein-like" creature, and the legion of servile spirits that they needed to keep in order then. In this regard then, we can likewise come to appreciate Defoe's use of the master-servant or master-slave relation as well as its enforcement for effective collaboration across levels of command and control with potentially defiant entities.

Last but not least, realize Defoe's framing of the deliverance of the savage\footnote{In fact, at a higher, meta-level of analysis, one could argue that \textbf{the deliverance was two-fold;} Crusoe delivers the savage from captivity and brutal death at the hands of fellow savages, but also, and though not literally stated or acknowledged, the savage delivers Crusoe from captivity to not just the island (as we later learn), but also helps deliver him from nearly fatal loneliness. Thus, it might help explain why, several times, if not from the onset, Crusoe openly acknowledges Friday's relevance, authority and honor in his life, many times preferring to not talk of him as just a ``slave" or ``servant", but instead as a [fellow] ``Man" --- almost an equal or such!}; it falls on a Friday, and Defoe's stressing of the point --- \textbf{``the Day I sav’d his Life"}. So, combining this entire analysis, and in summary, it starts to feel as though Defoe somewhat paints or wishes to bring the reader's attention to Jesus's status as not just a ``Master," but also as a ``Saving Master" or perhaps ``Master Savior." Of course, other analyses and other reviewers or critics of Defoe might think or say otherwise, however, the evidence here is strong, Defoe possibly had something more sublime in mind when writing this part of the Robinson Crusoe story.
