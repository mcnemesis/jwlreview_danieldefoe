\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a chronological literary review of Daniel Defoe's 1719 classic novel \textit{Robinson Crusoe} by his student and modern novelist, J. Willrich Lutalo, author of 2025 literary fiction novel, \textit{Rock `N' Draw}. It is compiled over an extended period of reflective reading spanning longer than a year; between January 2025 and May 2026. The review is structured as a series of dated notes, each capturing the precise moment of engagement with the text, accompanied by excerpts from the novel and personal commentary. This method allows for a layered exploration of the novel's themes, narrative techniques, and cultural resonances, while also documenting the evolving perceptions of the reader across more than a year of study.  

By situating the review within a temporal framework, the work highlights how sustained, reflective reading can deepen understanding of canonical literature. The inclusion of excerpts alongside commentary provides a dialogic interplay between Defoe's text and contemporary reflection, offering insights into the enduring relevance of \textit{Robinson Crusoe}. Ultimately, this review demonstrates the value of chronological annotation as both a scholarly and creative practice, producing a record that is at once analytical, personal, and historically conscious.  
 \newline\newline
     \textbf{Keywords}: Daniel Defoe, Robinson Crusoe, Reflective Reading, Canonical Literature, Chronological Literary Review
\end{abstract}

\section{Mar 4, 2025 21:07:17 - That Solitude Frees Man from Sin and Breeds Better Souls}
\label{SEC24}



\noindent
\begin{minipage}{1\textwidth}
\vspace{1em}
\begin{quotation}
{\ttfamily


Now I began to construe the Words mentioned above, \textit{Call on me, and I will deliver you}, in a different Sense from what I had ever done before; for then I had no Notion of any thing being call’d Deliverance, but my being deliver’d from the Captivity I was in; for tho’ I was indeed at large in the Place, yet the Island was certainly a Prison to me, and that in the worst Sense in the World; but now I learn’d to take it in another Sense: \textbf{Now I look’d back upon my past Life with such Horrour, and my Sins appear’d so dreadful, that my Soul sought nothing of God, but Deliverance from the Load of Guilt that bore down all my Comfort: As for my solitary Life it was nothing; I did not so much as pray to be deliver’d from it, or think of it}; It was all of no Consideration in Comparison to this: And I add this Part here, to hint to whoever shall read it, that whenever they come to a true Sense of things, they will find \textbf{Deliverance from Sin a much greater Blessing, than Deliverance from Affliction.}


}
\end{quotation}
\vspace{1em}
\end{minipage}


Of course, such thought could have been inspired by the nature of life led by astute monastics and puritans in Defoe's era or day. Additionally, take note of the ``fancifulness" of Crusoe's state solitariness; he is alone yes, on an island devoid of predators but with an abundance of prey available for himself, an absence of human neighbours nor human interference, and the freedom to be or do whatever he fancied! All that, in a pristine state of wilderness ought make one think; wasn't the author possibly \textbf{dreaming of a natural paradise or perhaps a tangible purgatory} devoid of physical affliction and fit for purging the soul of guilt [from past lives perhaps]? 


Such a state would indeed readily lend itself to supporting ascendance toward a better, more natural appreciation of the divinity in raw nature. This, especially given, Defoe ensured that despite Crusoe having survived a shipwreck, he not only had the chance to return to the very ship to retrieve items from it that would help turn his imprisonment on the wild island into a little paradise, but also that he likewise managed to bring from the ship both guns for hunting and heightening his sense of security, but also the Bible, the reading of which would later turn a previously savage Crusoe into a cultured, meditative and higher spirited man!


Hahaha, \textbf{Daniel Defoe was such a clever dreamer!} Also, makes this novel start to feel like a work in some sort of Christian ministry; a creative work of evangelism!

Finally, reading just up to this moment, a more abstract point of view could be that, at that moment, \textbf{Crusoe was experiencing some kind of afterlife}; that he'd actually died with the other men that couldn't survive the shipwreck, though, despite not coming across any other human soul or spirit thereafter, his would proceed to depart from the ship and instead settle on the pristine island. His failure to see any other ships as well as his continued survival on the island over the prolonged period being some indicative proof he wasn't living in his former ``reality" or ``world" anymore. But, perhaps this just one extreme POV. One needs read on so as to ascertain how factual such an analysis might be.


\subsubsection{Later Note}

Of course, being the kind of reader that I am, I denied myself the chance to look-ahead nor cheat by trying to find out how the story ends before actually reading it to the end. And so, as my original notes and analysis indicate or propose, I felt that perhaps, Defoe was trying to paint for the reader a picture of what Crusoe's existence in a kind of purgatory might be like, or at least, a kind of existence in a literal paradise in some sort of afterlife. 

However, and actually much later, I did establish that eventually --- after almost a quarter of a century in his extreme solitariness --- other human spirits, in fact, [fellow converts and] comrades, did join him, and that he eventually got delivered from the ``involuntary, purging prison" on the Island, finally returning to his real kin and friends in Europe --- a kind of ``rebirth" or ``resurrection" perhaps?. 

But still, and without diluting nor attacking Defoe's creativity and ingenuity, it is very possible that he had ideas of a paradise or at least an afterlife/life-after-death in mind when crafting this part of Crusoe's adventure.
