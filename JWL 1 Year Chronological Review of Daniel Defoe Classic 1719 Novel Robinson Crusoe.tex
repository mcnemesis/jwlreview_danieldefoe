\begin{abstract}
This \textbf{monograph} presents a \textbf{chronological literary review} of Daniel Defoe's 1719 classic novel \textit{Robinson Crusoe}. The review has been planned but is also written by his student and modern novelist, Joseph Willrich Lutalo C.M.R.W., author of 2025 wartime fiction novel, \textit{Rock `N' Draw} as well as the 2018 occult fiction novel, \textit{Shrines of The Free Men}. The review has been compiled over an extended period of reflective reading spanning longer than a year; between January 2025 and May 2026. It is structured as a series of dated notes, each capturing the precise moment of engagement with the text, accompanied by excerpts from the novel but also significant personal commentary on the text. This method allows for a layered exploration of the novel's themes, narrative techniques, and cultural resonances, while also documenting the evolving perceptions of the reader and reviewer across more than a year of careful study of the book.  

Further, by situating the review within a temporal framework, the work highlights how sustained, reflective reading can deepen understanding of canonical literature. The inclusion of excerpts alongside both analytic and critical commentary provides a dialogic interplay between Defoe's text and contemporary reflection, offering insights into the enduring relevance of \textit{Robinson Crusoe} in modern life. Despite so many other scholars having studied, annotated, revised as well as reviewed this and other works by Daniel Defoe, and yet, as one shall quickly establish, this review brings forth an entirely new perspective to appreciating Daniel Defoe as well as his \textit{Adventures of Robinson Crusoe}. This, especially coming from a seasoned reviewer of various [other] kinds of both creative and academic works spanning science, philosophy and the arts. Lots of otherwise usually hidden gems in Defoe's work have been given sufficient attention and treatment herein. Moreover, and ultimately, this review demonstrates the value of chronological annotation as both \textbf{a scholarly and creative practice, producing a record that is at once analytical, personal, and historically relevant.}  
 \newline\newline
     \textbf{Keywords}: Daniel Defoe, Robinson Crusoe, Reflective Reading, Commentary, Canonical Literature, Chronological Literary Review
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Daniel Defoe: A Brief Biography}
\label{SECDEFOE}

Daniel Defoe\footnote{The brief biography shared in this section is courtesy of Copilot \cite{copilot_assistant} and might not divulge all necessary details. However, those interested can as well consult other authorities, including the official Wikipedia page: \url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Defoe}} (c.~1660--1731) was an English writer, journalist, merchant, and political agent, best remembered as the author of \textit{Robinson Crusoe} (1719), one of the earliest and most influential novels in English literature.

\subsubsection{Early Life}

Born in London around 1660, Defoe was the son of James Foe, a tallow chandler, and Alice Foe. Raised in a Presbyterian Nonconformist household\footnote{It might be worth noting that, without even knowing or realizing it until the writing of this review, the reviewer's latest novel, \textit{Rock `N' Draw}, \cite{lutalo2025rock}, somewhat appeals to Presbyterians --- including an explicit mention of and several activities taking place at the ``Presbyterian Mercy Hospital" --- a possibly subconscious but significant shift away from a heavily Catholic-biased \textit{Shrines of The Free Men}, \cite{shrinesjwl}, their first novel.}, he attended Charles Morton’s dissenting academy at Newington Green. Later, he adopted the name ``Defoe'' to give himself a more distinguished identity.

\subsubsection{Career}

Defoe began his career as a merchant, trading in hosiery, wool, and wine, though he suffered repeated financial setbacks. He became a prolific pamphleteer and political writer, sometimes landing in prison for his controversial views. He also served as a government agent, using his writing to influence public opinion. Defoe is considered one of the pioneers of modern journalism, founding and contributing to several periodicals.



\subsubsection{Literary Achievements}

Defoe’s literary fame rests primarily on his novels:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textit{Robinson Crusoe} (1719): Often regarded as the first English novel, it tells the story of survival and self-reliance on a deserted island.
  \item \textit{Moll Flanders} (1722): A tale of crime, poverty, and resilience through the life of a woman navigating hardship.
  \item \textit{A Journal of the Plague Year} (1722): A semi-fictional account of the 1665 plague in London, blending reportage and narrative.
  \item \textit{Roxana} (1724): A novel exploring morality, wealth, and identity.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Legacy}

Defoe’s works helped establish prose fiction as a respected literary form in Britain. His blend of adventure, realism, and social commentary influenced later novelists such as \textbf{Samuel Richardson} and \textbf{Henry Fielding}. He died on 24 April 1731 in London and was buried at Bunhill Fields. He was survived by 8 children including Hannah Defoe, William Defoe, Magnolia Defoe, Benjamin Norton Defoe, \textbf{Sophia Defoe} and others.
